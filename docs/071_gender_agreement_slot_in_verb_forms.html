1 Introduction

Noun classes are a distinctive feature of East Caucasian and, as such, are present in the vast majority of the family’s languages. The number of classes varies greatly among languages, ranging from 2 in standard Tabasaran (Babaliyeva 2023: 5) to 8 in Tsova-Tush (Desheriev 1953: 136), excluding Udi and Lezgian which lack noun class systems.

Usually the classes are covert on the nouns themselves and are expressed through agreement. An example of a noun class agreement marker system, taken from (Magomedbekova 1971: 41), is presented in Table 1. In some cases, the vowel which follows the prefix in a verb root may also change depending on the class; these vowels, however, are usually not regarded as being part of the markers, and the changes occur simply due to morphophonological processes.

The verb is one of the word classes that mark class agreement (which is normally with the S/P argument), usually in the form of a class agreement prefix. However, in many of the languages verb agreement seems to be in decline, and only some verbs take agreement while others do not. This is a typologically rare phenomenon which is known as sporadic agreement (Fedden 2019).

The aim of this chapter is to describe the distribution of class agreement on verbs across the East Caucasian family, as well as to identify the different positions a class agreement slot can occupy in a verb form.

2 Results

All of the languages that have retained verb agreement express it mainly using class agreement prefixes, which are the basic form of a class agreement marker:

  1. Bezhta (Madieva 1965: 116)
    šugošova boχ j-ũqča
    horse.erg grass(iv) iv-eat
    ‘The horse eats hay.’

Many verbs that lack agreement still feature class prefixes, although in a fossilized form; in other verbs the prefix seems to be completely absent. Thus, prefixes are present in some form in all of the languages.

In some languages, the absence of class prefixes in certain verbs is explained by phonotactic constraints — only roots that synchronically appear to be vowel-initial contain agreement slots. This is the case, for example, in Itsari Dargwa (Sumbatova, Mutalov 2003: 60) and Hinuq (Forker 2013: 188). Even in such cases, however, not all vowel-initial roots may have agreement prefixes. In idioms where the distribution of slots among different roots can not be explained by phonotactics, the factors at play are less clear.

Sometimes, due to various historical processes, markers that would normally be prefixal take the infixal position instead:

  1. Archi (Kibrik 1977: 81)
    da‹b›xir
    ‹III›beat
    ‘to beat (something belonging to the 3rd class)’

We regard both prefixes and infixes as agreement of the verb root. However, in a number of languages agreement slots are also found outside of the root, usually only in certain verb forms (not counting participles, which have agreement suffixes in most of the languages). The most frequent non-root slot is that of a suffix:

  1. Northern Akhvakh (Magomedbekova 1967: 80)
    dede w-oxe-d-o waša
    i i-take-1sg-i boy
    ‘I took a boy.’

In this Northern Akhvakh example, the verb agreement with the word waša ‘boy’, which belongs to the first class, is expressed through both a class prefix and a class suffix. The latter takes on the form of a vowel yet acts like a normal class agreement marker; it appears in first person forms (while the person agreement is with the S/A argument, the class suffix still agrees with the S/P one).

In some cases, suffixes may function differently from prefixes. For example, in Kubachi the prefix always agrees with the S/P argument. The suffix, however, may agree with the A Argument; the choice of controller thus affects the voice of the verb (compare (4a), where the suffix agrees with the A argument, and (4b), where both the prefix and the suffix agree with the S argument):

  1. Kubachi (Magometov 1963: 153)
    1. tukančildil mas b-iːsulsa-w
      seller fabric iii-sell-i
      ‘A seller sells fabric.’
    2. ijal waxle mas b-iːsulsa-b
      today well fabric iii-sell-iii
      ‘Fabric sells well today.’

The other type of non-root agreement slots is that of an agreeing preverb, attested in languages of the Dargwa group as well as in Tsakhur:

  1. Tanty (Sumbatova, Lander 2014: 169)
    q˳aj-te χː˳ala-te dali ka-d=irxː-u-le dimʡaˁn-te
    massive-atr.pl big-atr.pl 1sg:erg down-npl=put:ipf-prs-conv small-atr.pl
    gu=d-d=urkː-u-le
    under=npl-npl=find:ipf-prs-conv
    ‘Big ones (money) I put (under the pillow) — small ones I find.’

In the example above, the verb b=arkː- ‘to find’, which itself has root agreement, is used with the locative preverb gu=b- ‘under’, which contains a gender agreement slot as well.

3 Distribution

Data on the exact proportion of agreeing verbs (in regards to type frequency, i.e. numbers of verb lexemes which have root agreement) in certain languages are rarely found in the literature. If any estimates are given in a grammar, they are usually rather broad. Thus, the available data are incomplete, although an overall trend can still be described.

As it has been noted numerous times by different authors (for example, (Talibov 2007: 186)), a tendency to lose gender agreement is present across the entire East Caucasian family. Indeed, the only languages that have seemingly retained agreement on nearly all verbs (except for some stative ones) are Rutul and Tsakhur, both of which are Lezgic.

In a number of languages (6 in our sample1), the majority of verbs are agreeing. In Hunzib, about half of all verbs have agreement. Among the aforementioned idioms (including those in which all verbs agree) are those belonging to the Lezgic, Dargwa, Tsezic, and Nakh branches of the family.

In Hinuq and Chechen, about a third of verbs agree. In the other languages for which data are available (7 in our sample), the majority of verbs lack agreement. Agul, Udi and Lezgian, as well as the Khiv dialect of Tabasaran, have lost verb agreement completely, with Udi and Lezgian lacking a noun gender system altogether; all 4 of these idioms belong to the Lezgic branch.

A notable case is Tsova-Tush: as stated in (Harris 2009: 298), 74.47% of verb lexemes have an agreement slot, but only 21.53% of verb lexemes have one in the root. Such a big difference in the numbers is due to the fact that certain derivational suffixes (producing intransitive verbs from transitive ones and vice versa) that contain an agreement slot are highly productive in Tsova-Tush, often combining with roots that lack agreement.

As for non-root verb agreement, it is found in a relatively small number of languages. Class agreement suffixes are attested in Avar and three Andic languages, as well as two languages of the Dargwa group, three belonging to the Nakh and Lezgic branches, and Khinalug. It must be noted that only finite forms were considered, as, for example, agreement suffixes on participles are rather common.

Preverbs with class agreement slots are found in all of the languages of the Dargwa group in the sample, as well as in Tsakhur which belongs to the Lezgic branch. Only Kubachi and Standard Dargwa have both class agreement suffixes and agreeing preverbs.

Overall, non-root class agreement is found in 14 languages, which are listed in Table 3 and shown on the map in Map 2. They constitute a little over a third of all the East Caucasian languages in our sample.

Class agreement suffixes are more common, appearing in several branches of the family, while agreeing preverbs are limited to Dargwa with the exception of Tsakhur.
