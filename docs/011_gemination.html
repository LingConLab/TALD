General chapter: Phonology

1 Introduction

Gemination is a phonetic feature, where a consonant is pronounced for a relatively longer duration of time (Mitterer 2018). This feature is not unique to Daghestan and is relatively common cross-linguistically (Blevins 2005). In the descriptory tradition of Nakh-Daghestanian linguistics, there is an ongoing debate on whether or not ‘strong’ vs ‘weak’ distinction (not the same as ‘fortis-lenis’ distinction) exists and if it is in any way different than simple consonant gemination and if ‘gemination’ is the same as ‘consonant length’ (full debate is summarized in (Isakov, Xalilov 2012: 72–76), at least in regards to Hunzib). Additionally, one may see the status of gemination being treated differently in different grammars. Nakh-Daghestanian tradition often sees gemination as a phonemic feature, so it is often included in the consonant phoneme tables (for example of such a description, see (Magometov 1970: 33)), while other descriptions may not view it as such (such as in the Azerbaijani description by (Širaliev, Sevortjan 1971: 16, 19)). For the purposes of this chapter, if in the grammar description consonant length or weak-strong distinction is mentioned we treat it as a presence of the gemination.

2 Results and Distribution

Most languages in the sample (38/58) have gemination.

Unlike some other features of the areal, gemination is not limited to the members of the Nakh-Daghestanian family, as it is also a feature of the Turkic languages of the area (Baskakov 1973: 9; Abdullaeva et al. 2014: 33–56, 72; Širaliev, Sevortjan 1971: 16–26) and Georgian (Hewitt 1995: 19, 26). Indo-European languages in the area consistently lack this feature and, notably, Tat simplifies geminates in loanwords (Authier 2012: 32).

Among Nakh-Daghestanian languages, gemination is consistently absent in the Tsezic branch and Khinalug. While Standard Dargwa lacks gemination, all other Dargic languages have it.

As for the Lezgic branch the distribution of this feature is less clear. Some of it can be explained genetically, such as gemination being absent in the Southern Samur branch. A Lezgian, Rutul and Udi lack geminates despite being more closely related to languages that do not, than to each other. This distribution can be explained areally, since Lezgian, Rutul, Udi, Budukh, Kryz and Khinalug are all neighbouring languages on the southern edge of the sprachbund, mostly located on the border of Russia and Azerbaijan.
