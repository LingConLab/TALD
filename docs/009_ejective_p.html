General chapter: Phonology

1 Introduction

Among the languages of the world with ejective consonants there exists a tendency for them to lack /p’/ (Greenberg 1970: 127; Maddieson 1984: 103; via Ohala, Sole 2008: 332). In the Nakh-Dagestanian family this too seems to be the case and thus, in this chapter the distribution of ejective /p’/ will be mapped across the languages of the family, as well as across languages outside of Nakh-Dagestanian family that are nevertheless part of the Dagestan linguistic area.

2 Results

As mentioned in the introduction, lack of ejective p is found in substantial amount of languages in the region, such as in Andi (Kaye et al. 2020: 4), Chamalal (Bokarëv 1949: 12, 15), Avar (Alekseev, Ataev 1997: 35) and others. But it is also attested in languages like Tanty Dargwa (Sumbatova, Lander 2014: 40–41), Kryz (Khalilova 2009: 13) and many others.

It is also important to mention languages in which /p’/ is technically present, but is marginal. In Karata, for example, /p’/ is found only in onomatopoeias like p’a (onomatopoeia) (Magomedbekova 1971: 14). Same applies for Avar (Alekseev et al. 2012: 31) and Kryz (Authier 2009: 12).

3 Distribution

Among the sampled languages all which are non-Nakh-Daghestanian had no ejective /p’/. This is easily explained by the fact that these languages, with the exception of Georgian, simply don’t have any ejective consonant sets. Same also applies to Udi, which belongs to the Nakh-Daghestanian family and whose ejective consonant set is in fact a set of glottalized stops, which includes /pˀ/ (Schulze 2005: 13).

Within the Nakh-Daghestanian family, /p’/ is absent or marginal in 21 languages and dialects sampled (of the 58 languages sampled). Notably /p’/ is present in all Nakh and Dargwa languages. Conversely, in all Andic languages as well as in Avar /p’/ is either absent or marginal, with the exception of Akhvakh. In all other cases the absence of /p’/ does not seem to be distributed genealogically, such as in the case of Khwarshi and Kryz. While for Kryz the reason is uncertain, for Khwarshi it can perhaps be explained areally, as it is surrounded by Andic languages, like Tindi and Bagvalal.
